Stories in the Verses

திருவாசகம் · கதைகள்
7 stories · woven across 51 chapters

Manikkavasagar never tells these stories from beginning to end. He drops them in a single line, a passing image, a name — assuming you already know them. But once you do know them, the verses open up completely. Each story is a piece of evidence he is building toward the same conclusion: if Shiva did that for them, how did he end up doing this for me?

Manikkavasagar's Own Story

The Jackals and the Horses

Appears in · Ch. 38 · 39 · 50 · and others
The miracle that started everything — and the debt Manikkavasagar never forgot.

Manikkavasagar was the chief minister of the Pandya king at Madurai. The king sent him south with the royal treasury to purchase fine horses for the army. On the road he stopped at Thirupperundurai, where a guru sat under a tree surrounded by disciples. The moment he saw him, something broke open in Manikkavasagar. He spent the entire treasury building a temple at that spot. The guru was Shiva in disguise.

When the king heard what happened he was furious — he had no horses and an empty treasury. That night, Shiva sent a herd of jackals transformed into magnificent thoroughbreds to the palace stables. The king was satisfied. But the next morning, when the horses were led out for review, they turned back into jackals and scattered howling through the streets of Madurai.

The king had Manikkavasagar flogged publicly. At that moment, every man in the kingdom felt the blow on his own cheek. The king, shaken, released him. Manikkavasagar never went back to court. He spent the rest of his life composing Thiruvasagam.

This is why the image of jackals turned to horses keeps returning in the verses. It is not decoration — it is the event his entire life turned on.

From the verses · Ch. 50 · Ananda Malai
"O Lord of Perunthurai who turned jackals into thoroughbred horses and made it ring out through all the world, who spread the sacred frenzy of devotion through all the great Pandya king's Madurai — O rare and precious essence, O Father, O Supreme Light too deep to fathom — I know not what to do."

Stories of Devotion

Kannappan the Hunter

Appears in · Ch. 15 · 18 · 32 · and others
A low-caste hunter who loved Shiva more than his own eyes — and Shiva who accepted every offering.

Kannappan was a forest hunter — low caste, unlettered, with no knowledge of ritual or scripture. But he loved the Shiva linga he found in the forest completely. Every day he brought what he had: meat he had hunted, water he carried in his mouth (having no vessel), flowers he picked with his bare feet. A Brahmin priest who tended the same linga was horrified — this was the opposite of correct worship.

Shiva, to show the priest what he valued, made the linga's eye bleed. Kannappan saw it and without a moment's hesitation tore out his own eye and pressed it to the wound. The bleeding stopped. Then the other eye began to bleed. Kannappan placed his foot on the linga to mark the spot, and reached up to tear out his remaining eye — his last eye, which would leave him blind and unable to serve Shiva at all. At that moment Shiva reached out and stopped his hand. He declared Kannappan the greatest of all his devotees.

Manikkavasagar uses this story constantly, always in the same way: he measures himself against Kannappan and finds himself lacking. "Seeing that I have none of Kannappa's love — my Father claimed even me as his own." The story is proof that Shiva accepts total love, not perfect ritual.

From the verses · Ch. 18 · Kuyil Pattu
"Seeing that I have none of Kannappa's love, my Father — taking my side even so — graciously claimed even me as his own, commanded me and called me with celestial compassion. Go and buzz to the one adorned with golden sacred ash, O bee!"

Vishnu Offers His Eye

Appears in · Ch. 15
Even the greatest god had to give everything to receive what he wanted.

Vishnu wished to receive Shiva's Sudarshana — the spinning disc of light, his most powerful weapon. He worshipped Shiva with a thousand lotus flowers every day. One day Shiva, testing him, caused one flower to disappear. Vishnu searched and could not find it. Rather than break his worship short by one offering, he reached up and plucked out his own eye to offer in its place.

Moved by this, Shiva granted him the discus. The story sits alongside Kannappan's in the verses — both are men who gave their eyes for Shiva, both received grace in return. Manikkavasagar uses them as a pair: if even Vishnu had to give everything, what does that say about receiving grace for nothing in return?

From the verses · Ch. 15 · Tiru Tol Nokkam
"The hunter Kannappan whose flesh-and-water offerings made Shiva's form shiver with delight, the Lingodbhava pillar of fire humbling Brahma and Vishnu, and the story of Vishnu offering his eye in exchange for the discus — shall we play the Thoanokkam?"

Shiva's Great Acts

The Pillar of Fire

Appears in · Ch. 4 · 8 · 14 · 15 · 21 · and others
Brahma and Vishnu searched for Shiva's beginning and end — and could not find them.

Brahma and Vishnu once argued over who was supreme. Shiva appeared between them as a pillar of fire with no top and no bottom, blazing upward and downward beyond sight. He challenged them: find my crown, find my feet.

Vishnu became a great boar and dug downward into the earth for thousands of years. He could not reach the base. Brahma became a swan and flew upward through the skies for thousands of years. He could not reach the crown. Vishnu returned and admitted defeat honestly. Brahma came back and lied — he claimed he had found the top, and produced a flower as false proof. Shiva cursed Brahma: because he lied, he would never be widely worshipped.

This is the story Manikkavasagar returns to most often. Vishnu searched with all his power and could not find even Shiva's feet. Yet those same feet came down to earth and found me. That contrast — the unreachable made personal — is at the heart of his devotion.

From the verses · Ch. 8 · Tiru Ammanai
"Even Vishnu the red-eyed one searched and dug the earth, yet could not behold those radiant, flower-like feet — yet those very feet descended graciously to this earth. He cut off our cycle of births and claimed us as his own in every way. Let us sing, O Ammanai!"

Burning the Three Cities

Appears in · Ch. 9 · 12 · 14 · and others
Three indestructible cities, one arrow, one perfect moment of stillness.

Three powerful asuras built themselves three fortified cities — one of gold in the sky, one of silver in the air, one of iron on the earth. They were granted a boon: the cities could only be destroyed if a single arrow struck all three at the exact moment they came into alignment. The gods believed this moment would never come and the cities were safe forever.

Shiva waited. When the three cities finally aligned — the one moment in cosmic time when it was possible — he drew his bow, held steady, and fired a single arrow. All three cities burned at once.

Manikkavasagar uses this image as a picture of Shiva's patience and precision. The power is not in fury — it is in the ability to wait for exactly the right moment and then act with total completeness.

From the verses · Ch. 9 · Tiru Porr Cunnam
"He who bent the great cosmic bow and with one arrow burned the three cities of the asuras to ash — let us pound the golden sacred paste and dance!"

Daksha's Sacrifice

Appears in · Ch. 14 · and others
A father's insult, a daughter's death, and an army sent to burn it all down.

Daksha was Shiva's father-in-law, and he despised him. When Daksha held a grand sacred sacrifice, he invited every god and king — but deliberately left out Shiva, as a public humiliation. Shiva's wife Sati, Daksha's own daughter, went anyway despite Shiva's warning. She arrived to find her husband mocked and her father refusing to acknowledge her. In unbearable shame, she threw herself into the sacred fire.

When Shiva heard, he tore a lock from his matted hair and from it created Veerabhadra — a vast, terrifying warrior — and sent him with an army of attendants to destroy the sacrifice. The destruction was total: Agni the fire-god had his hands cut off, the sun had his teeth knocked out, the moon's face was crushed, Indra was beaten, and Daksha himself was beheaded.

In the verses this story appears as a catalogue of Shiva's unstoppable power — verse by verse in Thiruuntiyar, each line naming another god who fell.

From the verses · Ch. 14 · Tiru Untiyar
"Agni's hands cut, Surya's teeth knocked out, Bhaga's eyes plucked, the Moon's face crushed, Daksha beheaded — drive it away! Drive it away!"

The Silent Teacher

Appears in · Ch. 12 · and others
Four ancient sages came with their questions. Shiva answered without saying a word.

Four great sages — Sanaka, Sanandana, Sanatana, and Sanatkumara — had spent lifetimes in pursuit of the deepest truth. They came to Shiva with their questions. Shiva appeared as a young man sitting under a banyan tree, surrounded by these ancient, white-haired sages. He did not speak. He simply sat in silence, in the posture of perfect knowledge, and held up one hand.

In that silence, all four sages understood everything. Every question was answered, not by words, but by the stillness itself. This form of Shiva — the young teacher facing south, teaching in silence — is called Dakshinamurthy, "the one facing south."

Manikkavasagar uses this image as one of Shiva's great paradoxes in the Chazhal chapter: a young man teaching the old, teaching without speaking, knowing without explaining. How do you make sense of that? You don't — you sit in it.

From the verses · Ch. 12 · Tiru Ccazal
"He who sat as a young man under the banyan tree and taught the ancient sages in silence — teaching all of knowledge without words — Chazhal!"
