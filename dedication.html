Dedication & Acknowledgements

Dedicated to

Mr. Chockalingam K. Chettiar

To my father — deeply devout, endlessly curious, and generous in ways that asked for nothing in return.

He took me to temples, photographed their sculptures and corridors with a quiet, devoted eye, and supported my mother's research work steadfastly through the years.

He let me break things — and stood beside me while we fixed them together. He never once stopped my curiosity. Without knowing it, he gave me something I carry into every new challenge: the certainty that nothing is too fragile to try.

And to

Dr. Valli Chockalingam

My mother — scholar, teacher, and the person who showed me that history is something you stand inside, not just read about.

She took me along to archaeological sites with her students. I was just a child tagging along — but those visits planted something lasting: an appreciation for what has been preserved, and why it matters.

That is how I have come to see Thiruvasagam — not only as devotion, but as one of humanity's most remarkable acts of preservation. For over a thousand years, with no paper, no pen, no digital archive, these verses were carried forward through human voice alone, passed from generation to generation.

Acknowledgements

The Seattle Nagarathars

My deepest gratitude to the Seattle Nagarathars, who gather regularly — far from home, across an ocean — to recite Thiruvasagam together.

Without them, I may never have encountered these verses at all. It was their devotion — steady, unhurried, continued in a new land — that opened this text to me and reminded me that the act of passing on is itself sacred.

And to

Umakaviya

My wife — my sounding board, the one who answered every question I brought from the screen to the dinner table.

A note

I am not a scholar of Tamil literature, nor a theologian. I am someone who grew up surrounded by temples, archaeological sites, and people who recite these verses with devotion.

When new technology made it possible for someone like me to attempt this, I did what my father taught me: I tried. I will break things. I will fix them. And I will pass something forward — however imperfectly — because that is what has always kept these words alive.

This is curiosity in action. Nothing more, nothing less.
