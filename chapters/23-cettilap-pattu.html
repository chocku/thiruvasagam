Cettilap Pattu

செத்திலாப் பத்து
Chapter 23 · Composed at Thirupperunthurai
← Ch. 22 All Chapters Ch. 24 →

Cettilap Pattu (The Ten on "Still I Have Not Died") is one of Thiruvasagam's most unsparing hymns of self-accusation. In all ten verses, Manikkavasagar cries out to Shiva at holy Thirupperunthurai confessing that despite all of Shiva's grace, he has still not died — that is, dissolved completely into God. He lists his own failures with brutal honesty: his mind is a hardened stump, his eyes are wooden, his ears are harder than iron, and he cannot melt in devotion no matter what he does. He cannot cut this body, cannot enter fire, cannot die, and yet cannot live in peace while separated from Shiva. Verse after verse he begs to be commanded to die, to be united with Shiva's servants, to be shown the great path. The closing verse shifts address from Thirupperunthurai to great Mount Kailash, lifting the poet's gaze from the local shrine to Shiva's eternal mountain throne.

I am false. Nectar flowed as my heart melted when you entered me, yet even now I have parted from your paired feet adorned with fresh flowers — I, the wretch, have still not died. Alas! I stayed awake and lost the very intention of my heart. O Master, O King, O rare and vast sea, O Father — O One with the red-bright form unknowable even to Brahma and Vishnu — I do not know what to do. O Shiva who dwells at holy Thirupperunthurai!

You are the anthill, the tree, the water, the wind, and the food — yet the beings of the cosmos and all others do not see your lotus feet. O King, you ensnared me with a single word and seized me as your own. Yet I do not tremble, my heart does not melt within me, I feel no compassion, and I have not destroyed this dull, unfeeling body. Still I wander. O Shiva who dwells at holy Thirupperunthurai!

You thought even this outcast worthy and bestowed your grace on me — with joy I walked on my head. O Bull-rider, O Sankara (Shiva the Beneficent) — O Eternal One who stands firm for all the countless celestials, O Eternal One who drank the venom from the churning sea, O Archer who burned the citadel of the enemies — please command me to die! O Shiva who dwells at holy Thirupperunthurai!

There are so many — those who became your devotees and strive in hard austerities, Brahma and Vishnu who melt before you like wax in fire, those who claim you as their very own. Why, with so many standing before you, did you single me out here? My mind is hardened like a stump. My eyes are wooden and my ears harder than iron. O Lord of the southern holy ford, O Lord of Shivaloka, O Shiva who dwells at holy Thirupperunthurai!

Leaving aside their own appointed duties, the dancing gods abide in the path of your grace, calling out "O Master!" through love — and so shall I. O Essence that the gods of the land and all who seek long for — O Lord, please show me the grace that never departs from you, show me your divine paired feet, and by your grace dissolve the illusion of this body. O Lord of the lords of even the highest gods, O Shiva who dwells at holy Thirupperunthurai!

O Shiva who dwells at holy Thirupperunthurai, surrounded by flooded fields where water flows through every channel!
