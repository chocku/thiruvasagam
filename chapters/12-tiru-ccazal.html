Thiruchazhal

திருச்சாழல்
Chapter 12 · Composed at Thillai (Chidambaram)
← Ch. 11 All Chapters Ch. 13 →

Thiruchazhal ("The Sacred Chazhal") is a dialogue folk-song in Naladith Tharavu Kocchagak Kalippaa metre, composed at Thillai. "Chazhal" is a traditional women's riddle-game: one player poses a puzzling question about Shiva and the other resolves it with a cosmic explanation, closing each answer with the refrain "Chazhal!" One woman challenges the other with Shiva's seeming contradictions — his ash-smearing and serpent-wearing, his coarse loincloth and skull-bowl, his forest-dwelling with no family, his acts of destruction and grace. Her companion answers every riddle by revealing its cosmic necessity: the four Vedas are his loincloth, the halahala poison his gift to the gods, the Natam his protection of the earth from Kali. The arc moves from Shiva's paradoxical appearance and poverty through his great mythological deeds — swallowing the cosmic poison, burning the three cities, teaching under the banyan tree as Dakshinamurthy — to a final revelation: whatever he wears, whatever he eats, his very nature is one who does not know his own greatness. The tone is playful and intimate throughout; the theology is vast.

He smears himself with white ash. He wears the surging serpent. He speaks through the sacred Veda-mouth. Don't you see, girl? Taking all of this — the smearing, the speaking, the wearing — that very Isa has become the inherent nature of every soul. Chazhal!

My father, our Lord — the Isa of all — why does he take a coarse-stitched loincloth? Listen: the stable arts and the woven meanings of the four Vedas themselves became his heavenly thread, and he draped himself in himself as the loincloth. See! Chazhal!

His temple is the burning ground. His fine garment is a tiger skin taken from the kill. He has no mother, no father — he stands utterly alone. Don't you see, girl? Even so — without mother, without father, standing all alone — through him the entire world is like ground-down stone. See! Chazhal!

He dealt unforgettable wounds to Brahma, Kama the bodiless one, Yama the destroyer, and the Moon. Don't you see, girl? When the master with three eyes himself punishes — is it not victory for the celestials? O you with flowing tresses, chazhal!

Daksha and Eccha · beheading · the assembly of gods · coming all together · them · destroying · what is it, girl? · coming all together · them · graciously destroying · then giving grace · to Eccha · a superior head · furthermore · graciously gave · see! · chazhal!

He beheaded Daksha and Eccha, and when the assembly of gods came together, he destroyed them too. What was that, girl? He cut them all down as they came assembled — then gave grace. To Eccha he gave back a finer head. See! Chazhal!

Brahma and Vishnu · not knowing · in the form of fire · below the very ground · reaching the universe's end · standing there · what is it, girl? · below the very ground · reaching the universe's end · if he had not stood · both of them · through their proud arrogance · would not have given it up · see! · chazhal!

Without Brahma or Vishnu knowing, he stood as a pillar of fire stretching from below the very ground to the top of the universe. What was that, girl? If he had not stood thus, both of them, in their proud arrogance, would never have given it up. See! Chazhal!

the mountain's daughter · on one half · having placed her · yet another woman · by water-force · into his matted locks · she who rushes · what is it, girl? · by water-force · into his matted locks · if she had not flowed · the entire earth · into the mouth of the abyss · plunging · it would be great ruin · chazhal!

Even as he holds the mountain's daughter Parvati on one half of himself, another woman — Ganga — rushes through the force of water into his matted locks. What is that, girl? If Ganga had not flowed into his locks through that water-force, the entire earth would have plunged into the abyss and been utterly destroyed. Chazhal!

with great commotion · from the mouth of the roaring sea · rising up that day · the halahala poison · who ate it · his cleverness · what is it, girl? · if he had not eaten the halahala · that day · Brahma and Vishnu · included · all the superior gods · would have perished · see! · chazhal!

With great commotion, from the mouth of the roaring sea that day, the halahala poison arose — and he ate it. What was his cleverness there, girl? If he had not eaten the halahala, that day Brahma, Vishnu, and all the superior gods would have perished. See! Chazhal!

The Lord of the small hall at Thillai, who joyfully dances facing south — this great madman delights in the feminine. Don't you see, girl? If he had not delighted in the feminine, O foolish one, the people of this vast earth would have reached heaven through yoga and departed altogether. See! Chazhal!

He who has no limit — he immersed me, the dog who approached him, in a flood of bliss. Don't you see, girl? The sacred feet that caused this immersion in the flood of bliss are the heavenly treasure even for the sky-lifting celestials. See! Chazhal!

O noble woman! What austerity is this — he adorns himself with sinews and bones, lovingly wearing the skeleton on his shoulders. Don't you see, girl? About the skeleton — listen! Tiger skin, loincloth, skull-bowl food, the burning ground as his home — even so, who here will become his servants, girl? Listen! Brahma, Vishnu, the king of the celestials — they, and the entire lineage of his devoted ones, are his servants. Chazhal!

Under the banyan tree that day, he sat and taught the inner meaning of the four Vedas to the four sages. Don't you see, girl? And yet — that very same one who sat under the banyan tree and taught dharma is the one who destroyed the three cities, foundations and all. See! Chazhal!

He dances the koothu in the hall, then wanders accepting alms to eat. Venerating even him as a god — what is that about, girl? Yes, about venerating him — listen! Even the four Vedas, which do not themselves know him, have praised him: "Our great Lord, Isa!" See! Chazhal!

The good discus that cut through the body of water-armed Jalandhara — how did he graciously give it to the good Narayana that day? What is that, girl? Narayana dug out his own eye and placed it as an offering-flower at the feet of Hara. For that act of devotion, Shiva graciously gave him the discus. See! Chazhal!

The sky-like spotted skin, the halahala swallowed as sweet ambrosia — tell me the cleverness of our great Lord who consumed these things, girl! Whatever our great Lord wears, whatever he eats — his very nature is one who does not know his own greatness. See! Chazhal!

To the rare sages sitting under the banyan tree, he graciously gave the four — dharma and the rest. Tell me so I may know, girl! If he had not graciously given those four, beginning with dharma, to those rare sages that day, those who seek to improve themselves would have no knowledge of the ways of the world. See! Chazhal!
