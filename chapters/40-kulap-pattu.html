Kulap Pattu

குலாப்பத்து
Chapter 40 · Composed at Thillai
← Ch. 39 All Chapters Ch. 41 →

Kulap Pattu (The Song of Rejoicing) is a chapter of ten verses, each ending with the same act: holding — laying firm hands on — the Lord who rules at the rejoicing Thillai (Chidambaram). The subtitle names the chapter's theme as "the uninterrupted nature of spiritual experience": once you have truly grasped Shiva, the experience does not break off. Manikkavasagar moves through a wide range of states — past sin, karma, the pull of women, the trap of birth and rebirth, the crushing of the pride of Ravana, the subduing of the five enemies — and in every verse arrives at the same conclusion: I hold this Lord; I have him. The refrain carries the full weight of the chapter. Kulaa (குலா) means to rejoice, to delight together; Thillai (the sacred Chidambaram) is the place where that joy lives permanently.

Having treated even the wandering ones as kin, melting inwardly, I became clear that the thing I seek is only Shiva's feet. With souls gathering in a great crowd, I bowed down — and I hold the Lord who dances at the rejoicing Thillai. Is this not so?

He melted my inner bones, destroyed the twin karmas, uprooted sorrow, purified the pairs of opposites, and entered within — causing everything that came before to be completely destroyed. I hold the Lord of love at the rejoicing Thillai. Is this not so?

Sweeping away this birth bound in name and qualities, cutting off all impurity — in the way that all devotees come together and drink the honey of Shiva's compassion — I hold the Lord who satisfies at the rejoicing Thillai. Is this not so?

Before budding on a branch, blossoming, bearing fruit, ripening, and letting the body perish — before going that way — so that my trusting mind may approach, I approach and hold the Lord of beautiful gold at the rejoicing Thillai. Is this not so?

The sacred feet that walked in the forest following the dark, coarse wild boar — those feet are planted on my head. Because of this, I hold the Lord who subdues the powerful play of the five enemies that overpower — the Lord who rules at the rejoicing Thillai. Is this not so?
