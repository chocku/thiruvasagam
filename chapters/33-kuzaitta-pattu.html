Kuzaitta Pattu

குழைத்த பத்து
Chapter 33 · Composed at Thirupperunthurai
← Ch. 32 All Chapters Ch. 34 →

Kuzaitta Pattu (The Song of Melting) is a ten-verse act of total surrender composed at Thirupperunthurai. The chapter opens with a refrain that gives it its name: "If you would melt and dissolve my ancient cruel karmic disease — protect me, O Lord! I, who am full of cruel deeds, find no certainty in my own striving. O husband of Uma, govern me! Must you not forgive when I err? O one with the crescent in your matted hair — is it just that when I cry out, you withhold your grace from me, your servant?" The ten verses that follow deepen this surrender step by step. Manikkavasagar's central argument is radical: since Shiva has already claimed his soul, his body, and his very life as his own, Shiva alone bears responsibility for what happens to him. The poet surrenders all agency — whether Shiva treats him well or ill, whether he punishes or graces, it is not for the servant to decide. The register shifts from urgent pleading to quiet joy: by the final verse the poet has seen Shiva's sacred form, his eyes overflow with rapture, and the melting the opening refrain asked for has come to pass.

I, your servant, thought that you had taken me as your own to remove all my suffering. O my King who shares your form with Uma of the slender waist — you said "Ah!" with compassion. But O Lord of Shivaloka, why leave this disease-ridden body unscathed? O Lord, if instead of calling me and taking me into your service you only punish me — even a single blow is enough to finish me.

Your compassion took me — a worthless dog — and redeemed me. Has that compassion departed today, O my King who shares your form with Uma? If you would take my mountain-sized faults as virtues — O three-eyed Lord of eight shoulders — how long have I been this ruined creature? Show mercy on me!

O Bridegroom of Uma with deer-like eyes! O King — you pushed me into this body of flesh, made me forget your glory, and caused me to wander. But knowing my ignorance, you yourself must grant your grace. O King, all I do is call out to you again and again, asking: when will the day come that you call me and take me in?

You know what is truly worth desiring, and you give all that is desired. You are the rare one sought by Brahma and Vishnu — yet out of your own will you took me into your service. You yourself granted me your grace out of your own will. I desired only that, and nothing else. And if I have any wish of my own, that too is nothing but your will.

Long ago, the very moment you enrolled me as your servant, O mountain-like Lord — did you not take my soul, my body, everything? O my Lord with eight shoulders and three eyes! How can there be any suffering for me now? Whether you treat me well or treat me ill — am I the one in charge of any of that?

You yourself, out of love, took me as your servant — me, the dog who is lower even than a dog. This illusory life is entirely in your control. What authority do I have here? Am I the one to decide anything? O one with the eye on your forehead — whether you torment me or place me beneath your sacred feet, both are yours to decide.

O one with the eye on your forehead! I have seen your twin sacred feet, and my eyes overflow with rapture. Day and night without ceasing, I think of nothing but those feet. The way to leave this body on earth and come to rest at your feet — O Brother! Must I worry about how or when? How beautiful it is simply to be your servant!
