Potrith Thiruagaval

போற்றித் திருவகவல்
Chapter 4 · Composed at Thillai (Chidambaram)
← Ch. 3 All Chapters Ch. 5 →

Pottri-t-Tiru-Agaval — 'The Sacred Homage Hymn' — is the fourth chapter of Thiruvasagam, composed in agaval metre at Thillai (Chidambaram). The poem is structured in twelve long sections, each a sustained movement of praise. It opens with the ancient episode of Vishnu searching for Shiva's feet and crown, a myth establishing Shiva's incomprehensible supremacy. The poem then catalogues the soul's journey through all stages of embodied existence — from womb to birth to the obstacles and attachments of worldly life — before arriving at the moment of grace and total surrender. The heart of the hymn is a long cascade of 'Pottri' (Homage) refrains, each naming a different attribute, sacred site, or gracious act of Shiva, accumulating into an overwhelming act of devotion. It closes with the triumphant double refrain: 'Homage! Homage! — Victory! Victory! — Homage!'

As Brahma foremost and all celestials rose in worship,
as the sages of four directions bloomed open with every sense —
Vishnu, the sacred tall one with his radiant crown,
who praised you and burned with longing to know your foot and crown,
became a fierce great boar and bored through the seven realms —
exhausted at last, he could only cry: "Victory! Victory, O Lord of the cosmic age!" —
yet even while praising, he could not see the pair of lotus feet.
And yet — in this wide ocean-world, those same feet are made easy to praise.

From elephant down to ant — through every womb without defect I survived,
through the inner karma of each birth.
In human form, within the mother's womb,
I survived the tiny contest of life's first sparking —
the duality of the first month,
the coalescence of the second,
the fluid deepening in the third,
the great darkness of the four-month,
the submersion of the fifth,
the seeping and spreading of the six-month,
the weighted heaviness of the seventh,
the hardship of the eighth,
the growing anguish of the ninth —
and the tenth: the ocean of grief the mother suffers alongside the child.
Through each year that followed, through all its crowdings and dwellings, however many —
morning filth, harsh midday hunger, night's sleeplessness, the sea-like exhaustion of daily life, the endless journey —
through all of this I survived.

Dark-tressed, red-lipped, white-toothed — women beautiful as dark peacocks,
their combined grace pressing close, intoxicating —
breasts rising free of the bodice, radiant, swelling forward,
the waist aching under the weight, spreading on every side,
so full that not a blade of grass could pass between them —
from the piercing plunder of those sharp eyes — I survived.
In the vast realm of this mad world,
from desire raging like a rutting elephant — I survived.
From the many oceans called learning — survived.
From the misery that comes wrapped in wealth — survived.
From the ancient poison called poverty — survived.
From the many lowly grass-walled roads of shallow religion — survived.

Then one thought of God arose —
a turning toward that reality which knows no anger.
At once, six crore powers of Maya launched their various illusions, each different from the last.
Loved ones and neighbors gathered to speak atheism until their tongues blistered.
The ancient cattle-herds called "family" clung, cried, panicked at every turn.
Ritualist Brahmins declared vows alone are supreme and waved their scriptures as proof.
Sectarian debaters shouted and quarreled, each certain only their own way fits.
Then Mayavada — the arrogant argument of illusion — came roaring like a fierce cyclone, whirling, striking.
And Lokayata — materialism — like a bright and deadly serpent,
reached out with its cunningly divisive poison.
Amid all this, how many more great illusions circled and surrounded —

Yet without slipping — not wavering from what they had seized —
like wax that has seen fire,
I worshipped. Heart melted. Wept. Body trembled.
Danced and cried aloud, sang and praised —
stubborn and foolish, refusing to let go of what I had found.
I became exactly that: love that never breaks, that has no gap.
Like a nail driven into green wood,
the soft oozing spread wider and wider, swirling like the sea —
heart softening, becoming yielding, the true body shivering —
while the world laughed and called me a madman.
I abandoned shame. I wore the village's blame as an ornament.
Without bending, I ran — having lost all cleverness, seized by divine bewilderment —
that very state becoming the supreme wonder, the highest path.
Like a calf's mind, crying and panicking,
I never thought of any other deity even in a dream.
Then the One of the Formless Beyond came to earth
and graced me as the Supreme Guru.
Not scorning that greatness as small,
I followed the pair of sacred feet like a shadow that knows no separation —
before and behind, without resentment, in that direction —
bones dissolving, melting, softening again and again, longing —
until the river called love overflowed its banks,
all good senses united, crying out: "O Natha! O Lord!" —
words stumbling, body hair standing on end —
hands folded like flower-buds, heart blooming open —
eyes filled with joy, fine tears welling up.
For those who day by day cultivate that unfading love —
