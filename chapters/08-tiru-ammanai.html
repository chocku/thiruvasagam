Tiru Ammanai

திருவம்மானை
Chapter 8 · Composed at Tiruvannamalai
← Ch. 7 All Chapters Ch. 9 →

Tiru Ammanai is a communal game-song cast in the style of the traditional ammanai ball-toss game played by Tamil women — verse after verse, Manikkavasagar calls to his companions to sing Shiva's praises. The central irony runs throughout: the great gods Indra, Vishnu, and Brahma could not behold Shiva through any searching or any birth, yet he came down freely, entered the poet's heart like honey and nectar, and claimed even the lowliest dog as his own. Composed at Tiruvannamalai, the Red Mountain, the chapter draws on Shiva's famous miracles — coming to Madurai as a labourer, receiving a king's blow on his golden body — as proof that his descent is real and unconditional. The dominant mood is warm, intimate, overflowing gratitude: the speaker is a devotee among companions, marvelling together at what Shiva has done. Twenty verses close with the prayer that we hold to the one thing that frees us from all other holding — the boundless bliss only he can give.

Even Vishnu — the red-eyed one — searched and dug the earth, yet could not behold those radiant, flower-like feet. Yet those very feet descended graciously to this earth. He cut off our cycle of births and claimed us as his own in every way. Lord of Perundhurai in the South, where the coconut groves grow thick — appearing as a Brahmin, the gracious one called out and granted liberation. Let us sing the flowing anklets there at Arunai! Let us sing, O Ammanai!

Those on earth, those in heaven, those in the underworld, those beyond — none of them can behold him. Yet he is easy and near to us! The great ruler, Lord of Perundhurai in the South, came as the beggar. He granted a path to liberation that had not come before, and entered my heart. He dwells there as nectar that is never enough. At the mouth of the wave-tossed sea where fish leap and dart — he is an ocean of boundless grace. Let us sing of him! Let us sing, O Ammanai!

Indra, Vishnu, Brahma, and all the other celestials stood suspended in the air. While they waited, Shiva himself came down to earth and bestowed his grace. Wearing his ear-ornament and sacred ash, he claimed us as his own in every way — the glorious Lord of Perundhurai, who enters our very thoughts. He mounted the horse and tore away our bondage. Let us sing the endless bliss he gave us! Let us sing, O Ammanai!

The gods who came from heaven — Vishnu, Brahma, Indra — withered in the forest and rose from anthills, yet could not behold him. But he himself came to me, the dog, and blessed me like a mother — patting my head. Flesh and hair trembled; breath filled me from within. He is the primordial sweetness like honey, the light of perfect clarity, come down from heaven itself. Let us sing those celestial, flowing anklets! Let us sing, O Ammanai!

Me — the dog, fallen to the lowest, with an untutored mind — the mighty Lord of Perundhurai in the South, the beggar, took up. He kneaded stone and made it fruit, drowning me in his flood of compassion and cutting away all my karma. The Brahmin one — entering Thillai city and reigning in the sacred hall — is the swift Bull-rider. Let us sing of him! Let us sing, O Ammanai!

Did you hear it, O friend — the cunning way he played his trick? He is the Lord of Perundhurai, surrounded by its strong walls. He showed us all things that had been hidden; he revealed Shiva himself. He showed us the lotus feet; he showed us the honey of his compassion. He dwells in the innermost self of those who ceaselessly meditate. He is both distant and near. He is the servant, the one who dwells at Perundhurai in the South. He is the Brahmin, the one whose half is the goddess. He is the lord who claimed us dogs as his own. He is the truth that is like a mother, the one who himself became all seven worlds and rules them all. Let us sing of the ruler! Let us sing, O Ammanai!

O you with kohl-dark shining eyes, listen! While Vishnu, Brahma, and Indra searched through every birth, he — by his sweet grace — claimed even me as his own in this very birth, and now guards me so I shall not be born again. He appears as the true substance within all real things. He alone is permanent and stable. He himself is all things; he is the liberation for everything. Let us sing of our Shiva, who is that ultimate Truth! Let us sing, O Ammanai!

Bangles ringing on arms, earrings swaying at the ears, dark hair flowing free, honey-seeking bees humming — he is the red one adorned with white ash. His hand does not cling to anything; he pervades everywhere. He is real and true to those who love him, yet unreal to those who do not. The Brahmin one who dwells at Aiyaru — let us sing of him! Let us sing, O Ammanai!

Born as an elephant, a worm, a human, a god — as every life form — dying and being born again, I was worn out. He came and stood, melting even my flesh, gladly driving away all my karma. Sweet as honey, as milk, as sugarcane — coming like a king, he graciously took me into his own service. Let us sing the flower-adorned anklets of the celestial Lord! Let us sing, O Ammanai!

He graciously waned the moon. At Daksha's sacrifice, he crushed Indra's shoulders and cut off Daksha's head. He knocked out the teeth of the sun racing through the sky, scattering the gods in all directions — gladly driving them away. He is the Lord of Perundhurai, surrounded by groves of red-flowering trees. Let us sing his garland of Mandara flowers! Let us sing, O Ammanai!

He mingled within me as flesh, as life, as consciousness itself. He is honey within me; he is nectar; he is a lump of sweet sugarcane. He graciously gave even to us the path that the celestials do not know. The servant adorned with honey-drenched Konrai flowers — full of glorious radiance — he is inexhaustible consciousness, the king standing over all the countless living beings. Let us proclaim it! Let us sing, O Ammanai!
