Tiru Ecaravu

திருவேசறவு
Chapter 38 · Composed at Thirupperunthurai
GratitudeWonder
← Ch. 37 All Chapters Ch. 39 →

Tiru Ecaravu (The Song of Marveling) is Manikkavasagar's litany of gratitude and astonishment. Each verse recounts a specific act of divine grace — drawing out a heart hard as iron, showing the sweetness of sugarcane, turning jackals into great horses, saying "Fear not!," entering the heart uninvited — and closes with the same rhetorical question: "Is this not so?" (அன்றே). The tone is not triumphant but tenderly amazed: how did this happen? How did one as lowly as I receive all this? The chapter moves through ten acts of grace, from the first miracle of being drawn in (verse 1) to the final wonder of God entering the heart of his own accord and bestowing the sacred mantra (verse 10). It is one of the most intimate chapters in the collection — less a praise hymn than a whispered conversation with grace itself.

1

You drew me in — me, whose mind was as hard as iron — and melted my bones. You showed me the sweetness of sugarcane and revealed your sacred feet. O Lord whose matted locks are roamed by waves! You turned all the jackals into great horses — is this not your great grace?

O Lord who shares your form with Uma, the lady of melodious speech! O nectar that fills those who have become yours! O Lord! You cut off my earth-bound birth and called out "Come!" — and I was saved, my eyes filled with the sight of your sacred feet. Is this not so?

Without any resource, I was sinking in the terrible hell of endless birth and rebirth, with no kin to help, crying out "Save me!" O Lord who swallowed the ocean's poison! You showed this servant your lotus feet — is this not so, O our Supreme Ancestor?

O one who dances with the green-legged serpent! O one with spreading matted locks! O Lord whose lotus feet rest on the crowns of the great! You took me, this servant, and saved me — so that I no longer praise the minor gods of others. O wonder! The way I was saved through my own mind's path, just by thinking of your greatness — is this not so?
5

I have not learned the arts or scriptures. I have not melted and dissolved in devotion. Even so, I know no other god. Through the natural flow of praise I came to your long sacred feet and stood there with confidence. O our Lord! You gave this dog-servant the golden throne — is this not your golden grace?
6

I fell into trouble through the sidelong glances of women with cotton-soft feet, trembling as poisonous sorrow grew. By your grace, I was saved. O our Lord! O one who possesses all! You said "Fear not!" and claimed this servant — is this not so, O nectar of the Ambalam?

O Lord Shiva who dwells in Thirupperunthurai in the south, unknowable even to the immortals! You cut off my cycle of birth, entered with love so that my heart softened, and took me as your servant, looking south. Is this not so, O our Lord?

O ancient one! O primal source that never ages! O endless, equal one! O the very substance of all things! O one who blossomed as both being and non-being! With compassion, you lifted up me who was rolling about here in this world and drew me in as your own — is this not so, O our Lord?

As your beloved lotus feet grow in my mind and my inner self melts, I go crying aloud through every street, praising you and saying "Lord Shiva!" Grant me, here, the grace to bathe in the vast sea of your great compassion that I have tasted and drunk. O Lord of Idamaruda! O Master who dwells in this place!

Did I myself perform austerities? I received the sacred mantra "Sivaya Nama." Lord Shiva, sweet as honey and sweet nectar, came of his own accord, entered my heart, and gave grace to this servant — to make me turn away and renounce this flesh-filled life.
