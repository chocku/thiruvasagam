Pirartanai Pattu

பிரார்த்தனைப் பத்து
Chapter 32 · Composed at Thirupperunthurai
← Ch. 31 All Chapters Ch. 33 →

Pirartanai Pattu (The Ten of Prayer) follows directly after Kanda Pattu's sealed vision of Thillai. Where every verse of that chapter closed with "I beheld!" — past tense, the seeing accomplished — here every verse is longing, want, and the ache of what is not yet possessed. The poet watches Shiva's devotees: they plunge into the ocean of nectar-grace, their hearts melt endlessly, they bathe and laugh and rejoice. He stands outside like an outcast dog, or like a mind of solid bamboo that cannot melt, or a withered tree, or a one-shouldered hollow gourd. Verse after verse he names what others receive and asks when the same will reach him. The appeal sharpens as the chapter progresses: first lamenting his separation, then naming what he desires — true love that wants nothing, the severing of self in supreme bliss, the tender golden feet — until the final verse envisions what he could be: joining, melting, overflowing, dancing, grace mingled so that bliss itself becomes everything.

Back then, mingling with your devotees, I was idly rejoicing.
The days that faded away came and settled into hardness afterward.
I have withered, O my Lord, longing to behold the undying flame of bliss —
I have blossomed in prayer: grant me your grace, so that longing may fill this servant!

Some devotees received your grace and were filled with longing; I am wasted.
I, your servant, am drowning endlessly in this stinking corpse of a body, through wrath.
Weed out the fierce karma that is mine and let your ocean of compassion surge —
O my Lord, grant that this servant's heart may melt unceasingly!

All the devotees plunged and sank into the great ocean of nectar-filled grace.
Bearing this body filled with darkness, I have been exhausted — you see, O my Lord.
Those with deluded minds, seeing me here, said: "the madman is coming."
O possessor of true love, I need to receive it — without fear!

I need it! I need it! O one who lovingly chose me among true devotees and made me his own by grace —
O ambrosia who removed this servant's hardships! O rare great jewel-pearl!
O one like the flame of a self-kindled lamp — can it be that even for this servant,
there would be only dwelling in supreme love, wanting nothing, needing nothing at all?

Among your devotees who dwell close to you, may I too truly join them.
O one who shares your side with the carp-eyed, blue-lily woman — through your compassion,
can there be even for this sinner, merged in the ancient ocean of supreme bliss,
the severing of soul and body, without knowing even what "I" is?

Your devotees truly received it — their hearts melting endlessly within.
Lying outside, I the outcast dog, am lamenting, O my Lord.
I truly want to receive real love — O one who never departs, never separates,
who never forgets, never stops — O immeasurable, undying ocean of bliss!

Those who beheld the ocean-like bliss all seized and consumed it.
Is it proper that I, the lowly dog, sit here, increasing in suffering, weary?
Without having told me "O Lord, you yourself will grant it" — I have passed on and wasted away.
Through your radiant grace, may darkness leave — O Light, now truly be resolved!

Without resolving, without melting, I entered among those devotees who appear when grace overflows.
I, with a mind like solid bamboo, stand and erode.
Grant also the love that your adorned devotees have for you — with your grace in abundance —
come quickly without delay and grant your tender golden feet!

"Without a single exception, you gave your unfading grace!" — saying this, all your devotees
stood there, never satisfied. Shall I, your servant, be weary like a stranger?
O Sivaloka who corrected thought through glorious grace and enrolled us —
you must establish supreme bliss as never-departing, O Great Lord!

O one who shares your side with the doe-like one, sweet fruit of those who bow before you —
