Poetic Forms of Thiruvasagam

திருவாசகம் · யாப்பு வடிவங்கள்
10 forms · 51 chapters

Thiruvasagam is not a single poem but a collection of 51 hymns each shaped by a distinct Tamil poetic form. Manikkavasagar chose his form as carefully as his words: a vast cosmic hymn calls for the long rolling lines of agaval; a dawn-waking song borrows the intimate cadence of the empavai tradition; a dialogue riddle-game takes the call-and-response structure of the chazhal. Understanding these forms reveals a second layer of meaning in the text — not just what is said, but how the form itself carries devotion.

Classical Metres

Agaval

அகவல்
Chapters 1 · 2 · 3 · 4
Long, flowing lines that pour forward like speech from the heart — the oldest verse form in Tamil.

Agaval is the oldest verse form in Tamil poetry. Its name comes from the verb agavu — to call out, to cry aloud — and the form carries that quality: long lines that roll forward without pause, building into a wave of praise or longing. It feels open and expansive, like someone speaking from the heart without stopping.

Manikkavasagar opens the entire Thiruvasagam with four agaval hymns in a row — Sivapuranam, Kirttit-Tiru-Agaval, Tiru-Andap-Pakuti, and Pottri-t-Tiru-Agaval — each running to dozens or hundreds of verses. It is the natural form for describing something boundless: Shiva's grace, the soul's journey, the whole of creation.

Example · Ch. 1, v. 1–2 · Sivapuranam
நமச்சிவாய வாழ்க நாதன் தாள் வாழ்க
இமைப்பொழுதும் என் நெஞ்சில் நீங்காதான் தாள் வாழ்க
namachchivaaya vaazhka naathan thaaL vaazhka
imaippozhudhum en nenjil niingaathaan thaaL vaazhka
Long live the sacred name Namashivaya! Long live the feet of our Lord!
Long live the feet of the one who never leaves my heart, not even for a blink!

Venba

வெண்பா
Chapter 47
Short, precise poems where the final line lands like a quiet revelation.

Venba is a short, tightly structured verse form. Each poem is small and self-contained, and the final line always lands with a quiet, decisive weight — like the last word in a thought that suddenly makes everything clear.

In Thiruvasagam, venba appears only once — Chapter 47, Tiru-Venba — as ten short poems. Each verse circles around a feeling — bewilderment, longing, wonder — and that closing line either resolves it or deepens it. They read like flashes of insight, brief and precise.

Example · Ch. 47, v. 1 · Tiru-Venba
வெய்ய வினை இரண்டும் வெந்து அகல மெய் உருகி
பொய்யும் பொடியாகா தென் செய்கேன்
veiyya vinai iraNdum vendhu agala mei urughi
poiyyum podiyaagaa then seygeen
The two fierce karmas may burn and depart, the body may melt — yet falsehood does not turn to dust. What shall I do?

Shatakam

சதகம்
Chapter 5
A poem of exactly one hundred verses — ten stages of spiritual life, ten verses each.

A shatakam is a poem of exactly one hundred verses. Each verse stands on its own — a single thought, image, or confession — and together the hundred build into something complete, like a hundred steps on the same path.

Chapter 5, Tiru-Ccatakam, uses this structure to trace ten stages of the soul's journey toward Shiva — from the first moment of awakening through doubt, surrender, and ultimately joy — ten verses to each stage. The hundred-verse form gives it a sense of wholeness: every stage is accounted for, nothing is left out.

Devotional Structures

Patikam

பதிகம்
Chapters 21 · 22 · 30 · 36 · 44 · and others
Ten verses, one sacred site, one act of devotion — the classic form of Tamil temple hymns.

A patikam is a set of ten verses on a single theme, addressed to Shiva at a specific sacred site. Ten verses, one place, one feeling — the form is focused and complete. Tamil devotional poetry is full of patikams; the Tevaram hymns of the Nayanmars are built almost entirely from them.

In Thiruvasagam, several chapters are patikams — most addressed to Shiva at Chidambaram or other temples. Each of the ten verses approaches the same devotion from a slightly different angle, and the final verse typically closes with a personal vow or prayer.

Pattu

பாட்டு
Chapters 17 · 18 · 23–29 · 31–35 · 37 · 40 · 41 · 42 · 45 · and others
Simply means "song" — the most common form in the collection, covering every mood.

Pattu simply means "song" in Tamil, and as a chapter designation it signals a lyric composition meant to be sung — as opposed to the longer agaval hymns or the structured patikam decades. In Thiruvasagam, the pattu chapters form the backbone of the collection: over twenty chapters bear this designation, each a sustained lyric on a single devotional theme.

The pattu chapters range enormously in tone — from the longing of Annai-Pattu ("the Mother-Song," Chapter 17) and the bird-messenger of Kuyil-Pattu ("the Cuckoo-Song," Chapter 18) to the warrior imagery of Arut-Pattu ("the Grace-Song," Chapter 29) and the wonder-catalogue of Arruputa-Pattu ("the Miracle-Song," Chapter 41). The form itself is flexible; what unites the pattu chapters is their lyric intensity and their suitability for musical performance.

Vinnappam

விண்ணப்பம்
Chapter 6
A direct plea to Shiva — not praise, but honest asking.

A vinnappam is a direct appeal — a plea addressed to Shiva asking for something specific. The word means "humbly bringing something to your attention," and the tone is exactly that: not praise, but honest asking.

Chapter 6, Neettal Vinnappam ("Petition for Release"), is 50 verses, each closing with the refrain "see that you release me." The poet piles up images of helplessness — a moth drawn to flame, a fish when the water dries up, an ant inside a burning stick — and holds them up to Shiva one by one, as reasons to let him go.

Ritual Song Forms

Empavai

எம்பாவை
Chapter 7
A pre-dawn gathering song — girls go door to door waking friends to come worship together.

The empavai tradition is a seasonal vow-song performed by young women during the Tamil month of Margazhi (mid-December to mid-January). Before dawn, a group goes from house to house waking friends to join them for a ritual bath in the sacred pond and worship of the deity. The form's essential movement is this gathering — verse by verse, more voices join until the whole community is drawn into devotion.

Chapter 7, Tiru-Empavai, is one of the most beloved chapters in the entire collection — 20 verses, each closing with the refrain Elor Empavai ("Come, girls, to our vow"). Composed at Thiruvannamalai, it moves from gentle teasing of a sleeping friend to pure wonder: Shiva is the light without beginning or end, the dancer of Thillai, at once male, female, sky, and earth. It is still recited in temples during the Margazhi season.

Example · Ch. 7, v. 1 · Tiru-Empavai (opening)
ஆதியும் அந்தமும் இல்லா அரும்பெருஞ் சோதியை யாம்பாடக் கேட்டேயும்
வாள்தடங்கள் மாதே வளருதியோ
aathiyum anthamum illaa arumpperunch chothiyai yaam paadak ketteyum
vaaL thadangaL maathey vaLaruthiyo
We sing of the great radiant Light that has no beginning and no end — even hearing us, O beautiful-eyed one, do you keep sleeping?
Refrain: Elor Empavai — "Come, girls, to our vow"  ·  → Read Tiru-Empavai

Palliyezhucchi

பள்ளியெழுச்சி
Chapter 20
A dawn-song to wake the god — and beneath that, a call for the soul to wake up too.

A palliyezhucchi is a dawn-song sung at the temple to wake the deity at the start of morning worship. The singer describes the world coming to life outside — birds calling, stars fading, devotees arriving with flowers — and asks the god to please rise and receive their love.

Chapter 20, Tiru-Palliyezhucchi, composed at Thirupperunthurai, has ten stanzas each ending with "O our Lord, please arise from your sacred rest." But it works on two levels at once: the god waking from sleep is also the soul waking from its own long sleep of forgetting.

Tasaangam

தசாங்கம்
Chapter 19
A king's tribute-song — ten royal attributes, each one reimagined as a quality of Shiva.

A tasaangam is a song of praise built around ten royal attributes traditionally used to honour a king: his Name, Country, Town, River, Mountain, Horse, Army, Drum, Garland, and Flag. One stanza for each, one complete picture of a sovereign. The form comes from ancient Tamil court poetry.

Chapter 19, Tiru-Tacankam, takes this form and turns it entirely toward Shiva — each royal attribute becomes a dimension of his grace. His "army" is the force that destroys ego, karma, and illusion; his "drum" is the primal sound at the root of creation; his "flag" bears Nandi the sacred bull. A king's praise-song becomes a hymn of devotion.

Folk-Game Songs

A remarkable feature of Thiruvasagam is the group of chapters (roughly Chapters 8–16) cast in traditional Tamil women's folk-game forms. Each form is a communal song-game with its own seasonal context, its own set of props (a ball, a drum, a swing, a pestle), and its own closing refrain inviting participation. Manikkavasagar takes these everyday recreational forms and fills them entirely with devotion — the game becomes an act of worship, the invitation to play becomes an invitation to let go. Each has its own lively, communal rhythm that makes the praise feel joyful rather than solemn.

Ammanai

அம்மானை
Chapter 8
A women's ball-toss game turned into a hymn — each verse tossed to the next singer.

The ammanai is a ball-toss game traditionally played by women in groups: one player tosses a small ball while others catch and sing. Each verse in the ammanai form ends with the invitation ammāṉāy — "O ammanai!" — calling the next player to catch and continue.

Chapter 8, Tiru-Ammanai, composed at Tiruvannamalai, turns the game's call-and-response into a meditation on a central theological paradox: the great gods Brahma and Vishnu could not behold Shiva through any searching or any birth, yet he came down freely, entered the poet's heart, and claimed even the lowliest as his own. The game's lightness and the theology's weight sit together without irony.

Example · Ch. 8, v. 1 · Tiru-Ammanai
செங்கண் நெடுமாலும் சென்று இடந்தும் காண்பரிய பொங்கு மலர்ப்பாதம் பூதலத்தே போந்தருளி
chengkan nedumaalum chenRu idandhum kaanpariya pongu malar-paadam bhuudalatthE pOndharuLi
Even Vishnu the red-eyed one searched and dug the earth, yet could not behold those radiant, flower-like feet — yet those very feet descended graciously to this earth.
Refrain: pādutuṅkāṇ ammāṉāy — "Let us sing, O Ammanai!"  ·  → Read Tiru-Ammanai

Chazhal

சழல் (சாழல்)
Chapter 12
A riddle-and-answer game — one woman poses a puzzle about Shiva, the other resolves it.

The chazhal (or sāzhal) is a riddle-dialogue game: one woman poses a puzzling or paradoxical question, and her companion answers it, closing with the exclamation Chazhal! — a word of wonder or resolution, something like "There! How's that!" The form's structure is antiphonal; it enacts the movement from confusion to understanding in a single exchange.

Chapter 12, Tiru-Ccazal, applies this form to Shiva's paradoxes: why does he wear ash and serpents? why does he carry a skull-bowl? why does he burn cities yet bestow grace? Each question is a real theological puzzle; each answer reveals its cosmic necessity. The tone is playful but the theology is serious — the dialogue form perfectly mirrors the devotee's movement from bewilderment at Shiva's contradictions to recognition of their unity.

Other Folk-Game Forms

பள்ளி · உந்தீபற · தெள்ளேணம் · ஊஞ்சல்
Chapters 9 · 11 · 13 · 14 · 15 · 16
Six everyday games — pounding, swinging, flower-gathering, drum-beating — each made into an act of worship.

Several other chapters in Thiruvasagam draw on distinct women's folk-song traditions, each with its own occasion and refrain:

  • Tiru-Porr-Cunnam (Ch. 9) — a pounding-song (cunnam = paste): women pound sacred spiced paste as an offering, closing each verse with "let us pound the golden sacred paste and dance!" The cosmos itself becomes the mortar, Mount Meru the pestle.
  • Tiru-Tellenam (Ch. 11) — devotees beat the thellenam, a small hand-drum used in communal praise, calling one another with "shall we beat the thellenam?"
  • Tiru-Puvalli (Ch. 13) — a flower-gathering song: women go out to gather flowers for worship, closing with "shall we pluck the flower-creeper?"
  • Tiru-Untiyar (Ch. 14) — a work-song using the refrain Unthiipara! — a traditional pounding-cry meaning "drive it away / rise up, be gone!" — each verse narrating one act of Shiva's cosmic power.
  • Tiru-Tol-Nokkam (Ch. 15) — a seasonal women's group game, each verse closing with "shall we play the Thoanokkam?"
  • Tiru-Ponnusal (Ch. 16) — a swing-song (oonjal): women swing on a golden swing and sing Shiva's praises, closing with "shall we swing on the golden swing?"

Together these six chapters form a gallery of everyday Tamil women's life transformed into acts of worship — the devotion is real, but so is the play.
